Intrinsic Value Calculator

© InvestingSprint.com

This calculator estimates a company's intrinsic value based on Warren Buffett's Owner's Earnings concept.

Owner's Earnings: This represents the cash a business truly generates that can be distributed to owners without impairing its competitive position. In this calculator, it's derived from: Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization + Other Non-Cash Items – Maintenance Capital Expenditures.

Valuation Formula: The calculator uses a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model. It projects these Owner's Earnings for 20 years into the future. For the initial 5 years, it applies the 'Earnings Growth Rate' you provide. For the subsequent 15 years (years 6-20), the growth rate is automatically halved to reflect a more conservative, mature growth phase.

These projected earnings are then discounted back to their present value using your 'Discount Rate'. The sum of these discounted future earnings, minus the company's Net Debt (Last Quarter), gives the estimated Total Intrinsic Value. This total value is then divided by Shares Outstanding to derive the Intrinsic Value per Share. All financial statement data should be from the Trailing Twelve Months (TTM), except Net Debt which is for the Last Quarter.

Error!
Net Income (TTM): This is the company's profit after all expenses, including taxes, over the Trailing Twelve Months (TTM). You can find this "bottom line" figure on a company's Income Statement or on financial data platforms like TradingView or Yahoo Finance.
Depreciation & Amortization (D&A): These are non-cash expenses that reduce asset values over the Trailing Twelve Months (TTM). You'll find them on the Cash Flow Statement (Operating Activities section). Check platforms like TradingView or Yahoo Finance.
Non-Cash Items: These are adjustments to Net Income that don't involve cash movement (e.g., stock-based compensation, deferred taxes) over the Trailing Twelve Months (TTM). They're found in the Operating Activities section of the Cash Flow Statement. Look on TradingView or Yahoo Finance.
Capital Expenditures (CapEx): These are funds used to acquire or upgrade physical assets over the Trailing Twelve Months (TTM). This cash outflow is listed on the Cash Flow Statement (Investing Activities section). Sources include TradingView or Yahoo Finance.
Net Debt: This is a company's total debt minus its cash and equivalents, typically for the Last Quarter. Calculate it using data from the Balance Sheet (e.g., Long-Term Debt, Short-Term Debt, Cash & Equivalents). You can find this data on TradingView or Yahoo Finance.
Current Market Cap: The total value of a company's outstanding shares. It's real-time market data (share price multiplied by shares outstanding), not found on primary financial statements. You can find this on financial sites like Finviz or Google Finance.
Shares Outstanding: The total number of shares currently held by all shareholders. You can find this on market data providers such as Finviz.
Earnings Growth Rate: This is the estimated percentage growth of a company's earnings over the next five years. It's a forward-looking projection, not found on standard financial statements. Useful sources for analyst estimates include Finviz, Zacks, and Seeking Alpha.
Discount Rate: The rate used to calculate the present value of future cash flows, often representing your required rate of return or the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).

Indications: Around **8%** for double-digit growth stocks; around **5%** for defensive, stable stocks with lower growth.
